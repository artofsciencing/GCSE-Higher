Biology

Higher tier Biology extends into complex genetics, hormonal control, and plant science — with more demanding 6-mark questions.

B
H Higher Tier Only content
CALC Calculation required

Core Concepts

  • Eukaryotic vs prokaryotic structure in depth
  • Cell cycle: interphase (G1, S, G2) → mitosis → cytokinesis
  • Mitosis stages: prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase
  • Meiosis I and II — crossing over creates genetic variation
  • Embryonic vs adult stem cells and ethical considerations
  • H: Cell cycle control — tumour suppressor genes, proto-oncogenes
  • H: Therapeutic cloning — how and why

Microscopy Calculations

Magnification = image size ÷ actual size Actual size = image size ÷ magnification Eyepiece × objective = total magnification Standard form: 1 mm = 1000 μm = 1,000,000 nm Resolution limit: Light microscope: ~200 nm Electron microscope: ~0.1 nm
⭐ 6-Mark Tip When comparing mitosis and meiosis: location, purpose, number of divisions, daughter cells (number + genotype), ploidy. Cover all 5 for full marks.

Mark Scheme Points

Meiosis — 4-mark answer
  • DNA replicates before meiosis I
  • Homologous pairs separate in meiosis I
  • Sister chromatids separate in meiosis II
  • Crossing over at chiasmata in meiosis I creates new allele combinations

Key Concepts

  • Enzyme–substrate specificity: lock and key vs induced fit
  • Effect of pH, temperature, inhibitors on enzyme activity
  • Bile emulsification — increases surface area for lipase
  • H: Competitive vs non-competitive inhibition
  • H: Liver: deamination, glycogen storage, detoxification
  • H: Interpreting rate of reaction graphs — initial rate calculation

Calculations

Rate of enzyme reaction: Rate = 1 / time (s⁻¹) e.g. reaction completes in 25 s: Rate = 1/25 = 0.04 s⁻¹ BMI = mass (kg) / height² (m²) EAR (protein) = 0.6 × body mass (kg) = g/day Surface area (alveoli): ~70 m² in adult lungs

Exam Strategy

⭐ Exam Tip Non-competitive inhibitors change the SHAPE of the active site — they are NOT affected by increasing substrate concentration. Competitive inhibitors ARE outcompeted by more substrate.
Bile emulsification — 3 marks
  • Bile emulsifies fat into small droplets
  • Increases surface area for lipase
  • Faster rate of fat digestion

Key Concepts

  • Specific immune response: B-cells, T-cells, antibody production
  • Memory cells → secondary immune response (faster, stronger)
  • Vaccination: herd immunity threshold
  • H: Monoclonal antibody production (hybridoma method)
  • H: Uses: pregnancy tests, cancer diagnosis, targeted therapy
  • H: Evaluation of clinical trial phases (I, II, III)

Monoclonal Antibody Process

Step 1: Inject antigen into mouse Step 2: Isolate B-lymphocytes (make specific antibody) Step 3: Fuse with tumour cell → Hybridoma cell Step 4: Clone hybridoma cells Step 5: Collect monoclonal antibodies Advantage: highly specific to one type of antigen (target)

Exam Tip

⭐ 6-Mark Question Describe and explain how a vaccination gives long-term immunity: antigen introduced → phagocytosis → antigen presented → T-cells activated → B-cells stimulated → antibodies produced → memory cells retained → faster/larger response on re-exposure.

Core Equations

Photosynthesis: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ (requires light energy, chlorophyll) Aerobic respiration: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP Anaerobic (animals): Glucose → lactic acid (+ small ATP) Anaerobic (yeast): Glucose → ethanol + CO₂ (+ small ATP) RQ = CO₂ produced / O₂ consumed Carbs: RQ = 1.0 | Fat: RQ ≈ 0.7

Higher-Tier Extensions

  • H: Light-dependent reaction: chloroplast thylakoids, splitting of water (photolysis), ATP + NADPH produced
  • H: Light-independent reaction (Calvin cycle): CO₂ fixed by RuBisCO, G3P produced, glucose synthesised
  • H: Limiting factors in photosynthesis — graphical interpretation
  • H: Oxygen debt — lactic acid removed by oxidation to CO₂ + H₂O

Exam Tip

⭐ Graph Questions On a rate vs. light intensity graph: the flat part means a factor other than light is limiting (CO₂ or temperature). Adding CO₂ at the plateau will increase the rate further. This is the key 4-mark interpretation.

Key Content

  • Negative feedback in blood glucose, temperature, water
  • Insulin (β-cells) lowers glucose; glucagon (α-cells) raises it
  • Kidney: filtration → selective reabsorption → urine formation
  • H: ADH mechanism — collecting duct permeability, aquaporins
  • H: Kidney dialysis vs transplant — evaluation
  • H: FSH, LH, oestrogen, progesterone in menstrual cycle
  • H: IVF procedure and use of FSH/LH
  • H: Plant hormones: auxin, gibberellins, ethylene uses

Kidney Structure

Nephron stages: 1. Glomerulus (Bowman's cap.) → ultrafiltration under pressure Filtered: water, urea, glucose, ions 2. Proximal convoluted tubule → selective reabsorption (glucose, some ions, water) 3. Loop of Henle (H tier) → creates osmotic gradient in medulla 4. Collecting duct → ADH controls H₂O reabsorption → concentrated/dilute urine

Exam Tip

⭐ ADH Higher Tip High blood solute concentration → hypothalamus detects → posterior pituitary releases more ADH → collecting duct more permeable → more water reabsorbed → small volume of concentrated urine. This is a classic 4-marker.

Genetics Content

  • Monohybrid + dihybrid crosses with Punnett squares
  • Codominance (e.g. blood groups — I^A, I^B, i alleles)
  • H: Sex-linked inheritance (e.g. haemophilia, colour blindness)
  • H: Pedigree diagram analysis
  • H: Mutation types: substitution, deletion, insertion → frameshift
  • H: Speciation — allopatric vs sympatric

Key Crosses

Dihybrid ratio (RrYy × RrYy): 9 R_Y_ : 3 R_yy : 3 rrY_ : 1 rryy ABO Blood Groups: I^A I^A or I^A i → blood group A I^B I^B or I^B i → blood group B I^A I^B → blood group AB (codominant) ii → blood group O Sex-linked (carrier female): X^H X^h × X^H Y → 1/4 affected male possible

Exam Tip

⭐ Sex-Linkage In sex-linked conditions, females can be carriers (X^H X^h) — they have the allele but don't show the phenotype. Males (X^h Y) with ONE recessive allele ARE affected. Always use correct X^H notation in exam.

Key Concepts

  • Trophic levels 1–4; energy transfer (~10% between levels)
  • Pyramids of biomass — always bottom-heavy
  • Population growth curves: lag, log, stationary, death phases
  • H: Predator-prey oscillations — graphical analysis
  • H: Species richness vs evenness; Simpson's diversity index
  • H: Eutrophication mechanism (fertiliser run-off → algal bloom → BOD increase)

Simpson's Index

Simpson's Diversity Index: D = 1 − [Σn(n−1) / N(N−1)] n = number of individuals of each sp. N = total number of all individuals D closer to 1 = greater diversity D closer to 0 = lower diversity Lincoln Index (mark-recapture): N = (M × R) / C M = marked in 1st sample R = total in 2nd sample C = marked recaptured

Exam Tip

⭐ Eutrophication Chain Fertiliser → leaches into water → nitrates → algal bloom → light blocked → aquatic plants die → decomposers increase → BOD increases → oxygen depletes → fish die. 6 marks = 6 sequential steps — don't skip any.

Chemistry

Higher Chemistry includes demanding calculation work, electrolysis half-equations, organic mechanisms, and thermodynamic reasoning.

C
H Higher Tier Only
CALC Calculation required

Core Content

  • Sub-atomic particle masses and charges (relative)
  • Electronic configuration using shells (2,8,8,2…)
  • Isotopes — same proton number, different neutron number
  • H: Relative atomic mass from isotope abundance: Ar = Σ(mass × abundance)/100
  • H: Electron shells → energy levels; emission spectra from transitions
  • H: Flame tests: Li=red, Na=yellow, K=lilac, Ca=orange-red, Cu=blue-green

Calculation Methods

Relative atomic mass: Ar = Σ(isotope mass × % abundance) ÷ 100 e.g. Chlorine: (35 × 75) + (37 × 25) = 2625 + 925 = 3550 ÷ 100 = 35.5 ✓ Molar mass = Ar or Mr in g/mol Number of moles = mass / Mr Number of particles = moles × 6.02 × 10²³ (Avogadro)

Exam Tip

⭐ Isotope Calculation Always show the full working: multiply each isotope mass by its percentage abundance, sum them, and divide by 100. Show units throughout. Losing the ÷100 step is the most common error.

Structure Types

  • Ionic lattice: high MP, conducts when molten or in solution
  • Simple covalent: low MP (weak IMFs), non-conductor
  • Giant covalent: very high MP, most non-conductors (except graphite)
  • Metallic: high MP, malleable, conducts — delocalised electrons
  • H: Graphene — single atom layer, conducts, stronger than steel
  • H: Nanoparticles — size 1–100 nm, large SA:V ratio, novel properties
  • H: Polymer properties: thermosoftening vs thermosetting — structure explanation

IMF Strength Order

Intermolecular forces (weakest → strongest): 1. London dispersion (van der Waals) → all molecules, increases with more electrons (larger molecules) 2. Permanent dipole-dipole → polar molecules (e.g. HCl) 3. Hydrogen bonds → N-H, O-H, F-H containing (e.g. H₂O, NH₃, HF) ∴ H₂O has unusually high BP due to H-bonding (100°C not −80°C)

Exam Tip

⭐ Property Questions Always link property → structure → bonding. "High melting point because it has a giant ionic lattice structure with strong electrostatic forces of attraction between oppositely charged ions, requiring lots of energy to overcome."

Calculation Toolkit

Moles = mass / Mr Moles = conc × volume (dm³) Moles = volume (dm³) / 24 (at RTP) % yield = actual/theoretical × 100 Atom economy = desired Mr/total Mr × 100 Empirical formula: divide by smallest ratio of moles Molecular formula: Mr(molecular) / Mr(empirical) = n multiply empirical by n

Titration Calculation

Example: 25.0 cm³ of NaOH, conc = ? Titre of HCl: 22.50 cm³, 0.100 mol/dm³ Step 1: moles HCl = 0.100 × (22.50/1000) = 0.00225 mol Step 2: moles NaOH = moles HCl = 0.00225 (1:1 ratio) Step 3: conc NaOH = 0.00225 / (25.0/1000) = 0.090 mol/dm³

Exam Tip

⭐ Higher Calc Strategy ALWAYS convert cm³ to dm³ (÷1000). Write the molar ratio from the equation explicitly. Show every step. In a 4-mark calculation: equation (1), moles of known (1), moles of unknown (1), final answer with units (1).

Half Equations

At cathode (reduction, gain e⁻): Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂ 2H₂O + 2e⁻ → H₂ + 2OH⁻ (dilute) At anode (oxidation, lose e⁻): 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻ 4OH⁻ → O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻ 2H₂O → O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ OILRIG: Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain (of electrons)

Industrial Examples

  • H: Brine electrolysis: H₂ at cathode, Cl₂ at anode, NaOH in solution
  • H: Aluminium extraction from molten Al₂O₃ (bauxite) — very high energy cost
  • H: Electroplating — object as cathode, plating metal as anode
  • H: Copper purification: impure anode dissolves, pure copper deposits at cathode

Exam Tip

⭐ Half Equation Rules 1. Balance the main element. 2. Balance charge by adding electrons. 3. Check: left electrons should cancel right electrons when combining. Reduction: always add electrons to the LEFT side. Oxidation: electrons on the RIGHT side.

Bond Energy Calculations

ΔH = Σ(bonds broken) − Σ(bonds made) Example: H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl Bonds broken: H-H (436) + Cl-Cl (243) = +679 kJ/mol Bonds made: 2 × H-Cl (432) = 864 kJ/mol ΔH = 679 − 864 = −185 kJ/mol (exothermic, negative ΔH ✓) Remember: breaking bonds REQUIRES energy (endothermic input)

Higher Extensions

  • H: Activation energy from reaction profile diagram
  • H: Catalyst lowers Ea — shown on reaction profile
  • H: Hydrogen fuel cells: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O (overall), with half equations
  • H: Comparing fuel cells to batteries — advantages/disadvantages

Exam Tip

⭐ Bond Calc Errors Most errors: forgetting to multiply bond energies by stoichiometry. If you break 2 mol of H-Cl, that's 2 × 432 = 864. Always multiply by coefficient from equation before summing.

Le Chatelier & Equilibrium

  • Increasing concentration of reactant → shift right (more product)
  • Increasing pressure → shift to side with fewer gas moles
  • Increasing temperature → shift in endothermic direction
  • H: Kc expression: for aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD, Kc = [C]ᶜ[D]ᵈ / [A]ᵃ[B]ᵇ
  • H: Kc > 1: equilibrium favours products; Kc < 1: reactants favoured
  • H: Temperature changes Kc value; pressure and catalyst do NOT

Industrial Processes

Haber Process: N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) ΔH = −92 kJ/mol Conditions: 450°C, 200 atm, Fe catalyst Compromise: higher T → faster rate but lower equilibrium yield Contact Process (H₂SO₄): 2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃ (V₂O₅ catalyst, 450°C) SO₃ + H₂SO₄ → H₂S₂O₇ (oleum) H₂S₂O₇ + H₂O → 2H₂SO₄

Exam Tip

⭐ Kc Questions Pure solids and liquids are NOT included in Kc expressions. Only gases and aqueous species appear. Units of Kc depend on the equation — always derive them from the expression itself.

Reaction Types

  • Combustion: alkane + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O (complete)
  • Substitution (alkanes + Cl₂/UV): CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl
  • Addition (alkenes): electrophilic addition to C=C double bond
  • H: Fermentation: glucose → ethanol + CO₂ (yeast, 25–37°C)
  • H: Hydration of ethene: C₂H₄ + H₂O → C₂H₅OH (acid catalyst)
  • H: Condensation polymerisation: monomers with 2 functional groups → polymer + small molecule (H₂O or HCl)
  • H: Nylon (polyamide), Terylene (polyester) — structure and properties

Functional Groups

Alkane: C-C single bonds, -ane Alkene: C=C double bond, -ene Alcohol: -OH group, -ol Carboxylic acid: -COOH, -anoic acid Aldehyde: -CHO Ketone: C=O (not terminal) Ester: -COO- linkage (from acid+alcohol) Amide: -CO-NH- (in polyamides) Ester formation (condensation): R-COOH + R'-OH → R-COO-R' + H₂O (acid catalyst, reversible)

Exam Tip

⭐ Polymer Questions For addition polymerisation: show the C=C becoming C-C and draw the repeat unit with square brackets and subscript n. For condensation polymerisation: identify the two functional groups that react and the small molecule eliminated.

Physics

Higher Physics demands multi-step calculations, vector components, and deeper understanding of fields, waves, and nuclear processes.

P
H Higher Tier Only
CALC Calculation required

Core Equations

V = IR (Ohm's Law) P = IV = I²R = V²/R E = Pt = QV Q = It (charge = current × time) Series: R_T = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ Parallel: 1/R_T = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ Energy from mains: E = P × t (kWh = kW × hours) Cost = E × price per kWh

Higher Extensions

  • H: Internal resistance: EMF (ε) = I(R + r); terminal p.d. = EMF − Ir
  • H: I-V graph analysis — gradient = 1/R; non-ohmic components (diode, filament)
  • H: LDR and thermistor — how resistance varies with conditions
  • H: Alternating current: peak voltage vs rms voltage; Vᵣₘₛ = V_peak/√2

Exam Tip

⭐ Internal Resistance The "lost volts" = Ir (drop across internal resistance). The terminal p.d. is always LESS than the EMF when current flows. At zero current, terminal p.d. = EMF. Plot a V–I graph: y-intercept = EMF, negative gradient = r.

SUVAT Equations

s = displacement (m) u = initial velocity (m/s) v = final velocity (m/s) a = acceleration (m/s²) t = time (s) v = u + at s = ut + ½at² v² = u² + 2as s = ½(u+v)t Free fall: a = g = 9.8 m/s² (≈10) Air resistance → terminal velocity

Higher Forces Content

  • Newton's 3 laws — action-reaction pairs are equal and opposite
  • Momentum: p = mv; conservation in collisions
  • H: Impulse = FΔt = Δ(mv); area under F-t graph
  • H: Vector resolution — horizontal/vertical components
  • H: Circular motion: centripetal force = mv²/r (directed inward)
  • H: Elastic vs inelastic collisions — KE conservation check

Exam Tip

⭐ SUVAT Strategy List all known values first (s, u, v, a, t). Identify which two you need to find or use. Pick the equation that connects three known values and one unknown. Always check: if thrown upward, at max height, v = 0.

Wave Equations

v = fλ (wave speed) T = 1/f (period) f = 1/T Snell's Law (refraction): n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂ Refractive index: n = c / v (c = 3×10⁸ m/s in vacuum) Critical angle: sin C = 1/n (when n₂ < n₁) Lens equation (H): 1/f = 1/u + 1/v magnification = v/u = image height/object height

Higher Content

  • H: Total internal reflection when angle > critical angle
  • H: Optical fibres — TIR, endoscopes, data transmission
  • H: Doppler effect — source moving toward observer: higher f, shorter λ (blue-shift); moving away: lower f, longer λ (red-shift)
  • H: Converging and diverging lenses — ray diagrams, focal length
  • H: EM wave hazards — ionising vs non-ionising radiation

Exam Tip

⭐ Refraction Questions When light enters a denser medium it slows down and bends TOWARD the normal (angle decreases). When it enters a less dense medium it speeds up and bends AWAY from normal. The frequency NEVER changes when light refracts.

Nuclear Equations

α decay: ²³⁸₉₂U → ²³⁴₉₀Th + ⁴₂He β⁻ decay: ¹⁴₆C → ¹⁴₇N + ⁰₋₁e + ν̄ β⁺ decay (H): ²³Na → ²³Ne + ⁰₊₁e + ν γ emission: no change in mass/atomic no. Conservation laws: Atomic number: must balance Mass number: must balance Half-life calculation: N = N₀ × (½)ⁿ where n = no. of t₁/₂ Activity ∝ number of nuclei

Higher — Nuclear Energy

  • H: Fission: neutron absorbed by U-235 → unstable → splits into smaller nuclei + 2–3 neutrons + γ radiation
  • H: Chain reaction: moderated by control rods (absorb neutrons), moderated by water
  • H: Fusion: ²H + ³H → ⁴He + n + energy; requires extremely high temp/pressure
  • H: Mass defect → binding energy: E = mc² (mass converted to energy)

Exam Tip

⭐ Half-Life Graphs From a graph: find the time for activity to halve — that's the half-life. Repeat to check consistency. After 3 half-lives, activity = 1/8 of original. After n half-lives: activity = original × (½)ⁿ.

Key Equations

Motor force: F = BIL (B = field, I = current, L = length) Transformer: Vₛ/Vₚ = Nₛ/Nₚ (for ideal transformer) Power in = Power out (ideal): IₚVₚ = IₛVₛ Efficiency = (Iₛ × Vₛ)/(Iₚ × Vₚ) × 100% EMF induced ∝ rate of change of flux linkage (Faraday's law)

Higher Extensions

  • H: Lenz's law: induced current opposes the change causing it
  • H: AC generator: rotating coil in magnetic field; slip rings
  • H: National Grid: step-up transformer near power station (↑V, ↓I reduces I²R losses), step-down before homes
  • H: Fleming's right-hand rule for generators; left-hand for motors

Exam Tip

⭐ Transformer Calc If Vₛ/Vₚ = Nₛ/Nₚ, and power is conserved: a step-up transformer that doubles voltage must halve current. Emphasise WHY high voltage reduces energy loss in transmission: P_loss = I²R, so halving I reduces losses by 75%.

Core Equations

E = mcΔT (specific heat capacity) c(water) = 4,200 J/kg°C E = mL (latent heat) Lf(water) = 334,000 J/kg Lv(water) = 2,260,000 J/kg Efficiency = useful out / total in (× 100%) P = E/t = Fv (power) Pressure × Volume = constant (Boyle's) p₁V₁ = p₂V₂ (at constant T) p/T = constant (Pressure law) p₁/T₁ = p₂/T₂ (T in Kelvin!)

Higher Extensions

  • H: Heating curve — flat sections during state changes (latent heat)
  • H: Kinetic model of gases: pressure explained by particle collisions with walls
  • H: Absolute zero (0 K) — no kinetic energy; extrapolation of p-T graphs
  • H: Work done on a gas = pΔV; first law of thermodynamics

Exam Tip

⭐ Latent Heat During a change of state, temperature does NOT change even though energy is being supplied. Energy breaks/forms intermolecular bonds. This produces the flat sections on heating/cooling curves. Must state: "energy breaks intermolecular bonds" for mark.

Key Concepts

  • Solar System → Milky Way → Local Group → Universe
  • Star lifecycle: nebula → main sequence → red giant → white dwarf (sun-mass)
  • Massive star: supergiant → supernova → neutron star or black hole
  • H: Nuclear fusion in stars: H → He; energy radiated as light
  • H: Red-shift quantification: Δλ/λ ≈ v/c (Hubble's Law: v = H₀d)
  • H: CMB radiation — uniform microwave background from early universe
  • H: Dark matter and dark energy — observational evidence, open questions

Red-Shift Equations

Red-shift: z = Δλ / λ₀ ≈ v/c Hubble's Law: v = H₀ × d H₀ ≈ 70 km/s/Mpc Age of universe ≈ 1/H₀ ≈ 13.8 billion years Evidence for Big Bang: 1. All galaxies show red-shift 2. More distant = greater red-shift 3. CMB radiation (2.7 K) 4. Abundance of light elements (H, He)

Exam Tip

⭐ Cosmology Questions For "evidence for the Big Bang" — always mention red-shift (galaxies moving away) AND the Cosmic Microwave Background radiation as independent lines of evidence. Two separate pieces of evidence = 2 marks minimum.
